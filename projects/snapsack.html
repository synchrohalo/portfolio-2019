Snapsack

I was entrusted to create a cloud storage app, with the task of discovering in what ways a new competitor could enter the cloud storage app market. The result was an app similar to Google Drive and Dropbox, but with the added functionality of labels and a warmer, friendlier interface.

The problem

Upon learning that users were not likely to sort their files into folders despite finding organization an important feature for cloud storage apps, I sought to find the answer to the question, how can cloud storage platforms be improved such that users will be more likely keep their digital spaces organized?

The solution

Enter Snapsack, a multi-purpose cloud storage app with a focus on organization. A labeling system allows users to visually categorize their files and folders at a glance. Filtering gives users the ability to sift through files that may be buried deep within nested folders or see all types of files and folders associated with a label.

Process

1. Research

Survey

I created a survey to learn about cloud storage apps and their users. I asked questions about which apps participants use and which features they found most important and used most frequently.

Survey highlights

100%

use Google Drive, whether or not with another app.

87.5%

see Uploading Files as the most important feature.

69.2%

use Content Creation Frequently or Very Frequently, making it the most frequently used feature.

62.5%

see organizing content as Important, despite the fact that a larger amount of people hardly organize their files.

I found myself fixated on the last point. It seemed that users were out of the habit of keeping their digital spaces organized.

Competitive Analysis

To scope out the competition I selected two cloud storage platforms I was familiar with: Google Drive and Dropbox. The two function similarly, with the main differentiating factor being that Dropbox sees itself as a productivity tool rather than a storage app.

I also saw that there was no way to organize files in any other way than by moving them between folders (and by selecting folder colors in Drive). Could I improve a cloud storage app on this front? This fact was pertinent to my growing interest in how to encourage people to organize their digital spaces.

User stories & flows

Now I needed to determine which tasks were most vital to include in the app, based on which features people use frequently and which features they consider important.

It was during this step that I began ideating ways to help with the organization. I thought about the current problems with folders and the benefits of using color-coded labels.

Problems with folders

  1. Hard to access files deeply nested within many folders.

  2. Can only associate a file with one folder and its encapsulating folders.

Benefits of labels

  1. Provide visual cues with the use of color.

  2. Files/folders can be associated with more than one label.

I drafted this filtering user flow based on the the labelling system I was starting to develop. The idea was to used color-coded labels so that users could have a visual cue with which to identify the files and folders on their app.

2. Low-fidelity

Sketches

Now that I’d prioritized my user stories and flows, I decided that designing the dashboard first was of the utmost importance. The dashboard is essentially the user’s mission control center. This is where all the action happens.

After creating about 12 quick roughs, I put together a more “refined” sketch based on which elements and layouts I liked best.

Refined dashboard sketch

Features

  • Tab-like navigation

  • Contextual menu containing actions appears on the right on file/folder select

  • Filter files by labels by typing in a label name in the top right corner

I interviewed a peer for feedback on this layout.

Comments on the current layout

  • "I like the idea of having functions on the bottom. It's not fighting your title, search bar, etc.” (on the location of the “Upload file” button)

  • “What I hate about Google Drive is that it gathers all of its functionality on one side. Makes it hard to navigate.”

Comments on organization

  • “It bothers me that my files are not organized.”

  • “It would be nice to organize on the fly. As you upload, you can select tags for each of them.”

  • “I think labels along with folders would be useful. Let's suppose I'm working on project, but I don't want to organize by client, I want to organize by date or by sprints. Each sprint would have its own folder, but each folder would have different labels for clients/projects.” (on how you could use labels)

Wireframes

I started working on digital wireframes based on the previous suggestions. I moved all of the the upload and create functions into a button labeled “Add to my Snapsack” and placed it in the bottom right corner for easy access.

I also shifted around some of the other elements on the menu bar. During this process, I had the idea of displaying the labels in a list à la Projects on the app Todoist:

I wanted the labels to be visible so that the user had a visual key with which to reference the labels attached to their files and folders. The user would be able to check off labels on the side to filter their view. Because this would take an indefinite amount of vertical space, I switched Filter by label with the contextual file menu so that it would have more space to expand.

The side menu was looking a bit empty with just Filter by label, so I decided to move the user profile picture and storage space to the top right, giving the top left of the dashboard more breathing room.

Dashboard wireframe

Main changes

  • Upload/create functions placed within “Add to my Snapsack” button in the bottom right corner

  • Filter by label is now shown as a list

  • Folders are displayed first

As I started considering the labeling functionality, some questions came to mind. Would the user be limited to filtering their files with only one label? What if the user wanted to filter by multiple labels?

While digging deeper into those scenarios, I ran into a problem. Would selecting two labels mean that the user wants to see files/folders that include either label or would they prefer to see files/folders that include BOTH labels? I threw both of these options into the mix.

Something else I thought about was how labels relate to folders. Would a file necessarily have the same labels as its parent folders? Not so, but would a user like the option to do that as soon as they add a file to a folder? Perhaps. I added that feature as well.

I tested my wireframes with 3 participants, which revealed one commonality — that the labeling interactions needed some work:

Labels feedback

  • “It feels like it should be in the main UI section (top) where search and sort are.” (on where Filter by label should be located)

  • “Sending someone far off to the side of the screen is strange.” (on creating a new label)

  • “Even though I like seeing the list of labels on the side, I suppose it’s not scalable.” (on the vertical expansion of the labels list)

  • “Use the law of proximity when you can. Add label should be an immediate option next to or over the item in question.” (on adding a label to a file/folder)

It was back to the drawing board for me. I proceeded to sketch more solutions for the label functions.

I started to consider how editing labels for a file/folder would work. I imagined a small popup next to the selected file might do the trick.

I moved Filter by label to the top in proximity with the search bar because they have similar functions. Because of this, the sidebar was beginning to look too empty. I then thought it would be a perfect place to position the upload functions. I removed the “Add to my Snapsack” button and placed all of the upload/new functions in the bottom left corner as a list.

Upon this change, a peer commented, “It’s nice to be able to see the upload options. It’s more explicit this way.” With this approval, I moved on to working on high-fidelity mockups.

3. High-fidelity

Branding

I developed the brand around the word Snapsack, a potential app name I had in mind early on. The name Snapsack is a play on the word “knapsack” and the phrase “It’s a snap”, a reference to the simplicity of the app and how easily accessible files and folders are with the new labeling system.

The idea of the backpack lent itself to a few metaphors to cloud storage: a good backpack stores your belongings securely and is meant to be taken with you anywhere. The backpack became the logo, and the user’s storage space became their “Snapsack”.

I began making more associations with the backpack, my first thoughts being that of traveling, adventure, and nature. Imagery of the outdoors were forming in my head. It was at this time that I decided the product should feel warm and welcoming; nature isn't always cold and solitary, just as digital products for businesses don't have to be cool and corporate.

I also illustrated a set of landing page graphics to accompany the nature-themed and friendly mood carried by the Snapsack brand.

Mockups

With the branding guide in place, I could now successfully begin on my mockups. The first thing I did was to apply the various changes I needed to make from testing the wireframes, mostly a rearrangement of a few features.

Desktop dashboard

Main changes

  • Filter by label moved to top near navigation and above files

  • Removal of “Add to Snapsack” button in favor of placing all upload actions on the sidebar

  • Prompt user to choose a folder upon upload

I applied Material Design principles for Snapsack’s mobile web app, using a floating action button for the Upload/Create functions and a navigation drawer for access to different parts of the screen.

Mobile dashboard

I tested my high-fidelity prototypes with three individuals, one remotely and two in person, asking them to complete several tasks.

“Make your page in such a way that I can scan it in 3 seconds. [When] actions appear on the other side [it’s confusing],” said one of my participants on how to manipulate a file. This was consistent with earlier wireframe problems regarding the proximity of Edit labels with the files.

Another interesting point brought up during testing was that using multiple labels to filter files was probably not a common use case. I admit I might’ve been thinking about it in terms of other disciplines, imagining files as databases that might need a distinction between a union or selection of queried data. This is definitely something to examine in future iterations.

Despite these criticisms, though, I received some positive comments regarding the organizational features of Snapsack:

On labeling

  • “This looks like it has a lot of color, colored labels, categories, which help me to organize better.”

  • “It would make me more likely to use [a labeling system]. Your design is nice and easy to use.”

  • “I would totally use this app. I’ve always wanted to use labels to help me organize my files.”

On selecting a folder upon upload

  • “I feel that if it didn’t ask me to [choose a folder], I would’ve been lost. It makes sense that it asks me to choose a folder.”

  • “I like this idea of having to select a folder so I know where I’m uploading the file to.”

Final

In previous iterations, I failed to use proximity to guide users in making an association between the target item and its actions.

This was a recurring issue, whether it was about where Filter by label should go or where Edit labels should be placed. As such, I moved the file menu in a popup closer to the selected file.

Conclusion

Organization in cloud storage apps can be improved. There is potential for labels to become a useful tool in terms of how people sort their files and folders.

Next steps

Moving forward, the specifics of filtering with labels needs a bit more research. Would users ever need to use more than one label to locate what they need? If so, how often would this use case even apply?

What I learned

  • Establishing goals for survey writing is important. “Cloud storage app” is a vague term, and my survey reflected that I, too, was unsure of which direction to take with my project. As a result, my survey was weak, and the results I got revealed too many gaps of information that I had to “fill in” myself by making assumptions about my audience.

  • Proximity is key. People are more likely to associate two separate items if they are in close proximity with one another. It’s a basic principle of design, but it can be easy to forget when dealing with a large application.

  • Don’t use too many components in Figma. Though specific to the design tool I used, this proved to be detrimental to my progress, as Figma was not meant to handle this amount of components and nesting. When designing my high-fidelity mockups, I nested components within components so that my working file would be as modular as possible (think classes in object-oriented programming). As a result, copying one screen took the app upwards of 2 minutes to complete, and loading each screen of my prototype was painfully slow as well.

What I would do differently

  • Focus on the organization aspect of the app. I got too caught up in other features like notes and previewing files that weren’t really fleshed out in the end.

  • Conduct more follow-up research about cloud storage app users and their organization habits. I feel that my survey data wasn’t sufficient due to low participant numbers and lack of foresight regarding the direction the product would take.

  • Consider options on making labels accessible. At the moment, labels are entirely reliant on the user’s ability to perceive colors accurately.

In spite of the setbacks, the positive comments I received post-testing reaffirmed that I was doing something right. A labeling system is a relatively simple feature that cloud storage apps could use to encourage their users to keep their digital spaces neat. Folders are “enough” but provide very little in the way of visual cues or non-linear access to files. Filtering by labels could be the answer to finding files easily.
