Cloud storage app

I was entrusted to create a cloud storage app, with the task of discovering in what ways a new competitor could enter the cloud storage app market. The result was an app similar to Google Drive and Dropbox, but with the added functionality of labels and a warmer, friendlier interface.

Summary

Type

Self-directed solo project

Roles

UX Designer, UI Designer, Illustrator

Tools

Paper & pen, Figma, Photoshop, Illustrator, Krita Paint

Deliverables

User surveys, Competitive analysis, User personas, User stories, User flows, Sitemap, Low-fidelity wireframes, High-fidelity mockups, Branding guide, Graphic illustration

The problem

Upon learning that users were not likely to sort their files into folders despite finding organization an important feature for cloud storage apps, I sought to find the answer to the question, how can cloud storage platforms be improved such that users will be more likely keep their digital spaces organized?

The solution

Enter Snapsack, a multi-purpose cloud storage app with a focus on organization. A labeling system allows users to visually categorize their files and folders at a glance. Filtering gives users the ability to sift through files that may be buried deep within nested folders or see all types of files and folders associated with a label.

Process

01 Research

User survey

I created a survey to learn about cloud storage apps and their users. I asked questions about which apps participants use and which features they found most important and used most frequently.

I found myself fixated on the last point. It seemed that users were out of the habit of keeping their digital spaces organized.

Competitive analysis

To scope out the competition I selected two cloud storage platforms with which I was familiar: Google Drive and Dropbox.

Takeaways

The two function similarly, with the main differentiating factor being that Dropbox sees itself as a productivity tool rather than a storage app. Furthermore, the only way to organize files is by moving them between folders (and by selecting folder colors in Drive).

Could I improve a cloud storage app in terms of organization? This discovery was pertinent to my growing interest in how to encourage people to organize their digital spaces.

User stories

The most impactful user stories I came up with, in order of priority (based on what people use frequently and rated important) and relevance, would make up the MVP of my product:

Minimum viable product

  • Upload individual files

  • Upload a group of files from my computer and mobile device

  • Access and recover deleted files

  • Create a document from scratch

  • Leave comments on files

  • Organize my files in folders or by tags/labels

  • Share a file with others

  • View the amount of available storage space left

User flows

It was during this stage that I played around more with the idea of labels. I designed my flows under the assumption that folders were a limiting way of navigation and that some sort of labeling or tagging system could help solve two problems that I had with folders:

Problems with folders

  1. Hard to access files deeply nested within many folders.

  2. Can only associate a file with one folder and its encapsulating folders.

I also thought about the benefits of using color-coded labels:

Benefits of labels

  1. Provide visual cues with the use of color.

  2. Files/folders can be associated with more than one label.

I drafted the following user flows based on the the labeling system developing in my mind. The idea was to used color-coded labels so that users could have a visual cue with which to identify the files and folders on their app.

Sitemap

I based the sitemap on my user flows, making sure each action was accessible from the dashboard.

02 Low-fidelity

Sketches

Now that I’d prioritized my user stories and flows, I decided that designing the dashboard first was of the utmost importance. The dashboard is essentially the user’s mission control center. This is where all the action happens.

After creating about 12 quick roughs, I put together a more “refined” sketch based on which elements and layouts I liked best.

Refined dashboard

Main features

  • Tab-like navigation

  • Contextual menu containing actions appears on the right on file/folder select.

  • Filter files by labels by typing in a label name in the top right corner.

Testing my sketches

I interviewed a peer for feedback on this dashboard layout and to gauge interest in a labeling system.

Takeaways

  • Upload button could be more accessible in the bottom right corner.

  • Google Drive concentrates functionality on one side, which could make it hard to navigate.

  • A labeling system could be used to categorize files within a folder.

It bothers me that my files are not organized....[but] I’m too lazy to [do it].

- Tester on how he feels about organization

The quote above prompted me to believe that users might need encouragement to organize their files. Drive does not ask users to choose a folder upon upload -- it automatically uploads the file to where the user has navigated.

Testing my wireframes

I tested my wireframes with 3 participants, which revealed one commonality -- that the labeling interactions needed some work.

Grouping by association

Filter by label is associated with searching and should be grouped with actions similar to it.

Law of proximity

03 High-fidelity

Branding

I worked on the branding concurrently with the the wireframes. I developed the brand around the word Snapsack, a play on the word “knapsack” and the phrase “It’s a snap”, referencing the simplicity of the app and how easily accessible files and folders are with the new labeling system.

The mark

I drew a few parallels between the idea of a backpack and cloud storage: a good backpack stores your belongings securely and is meant to be taken with you anywhere. The backpack became the logo, and the user’s storage space became their “Snapsack”.

Illustration style

After reading the testers’ comments, I realized that most people who chose B did so because the select state was more “obvious”. Perhaps I changed too many variables for this particular test, but I came to the conclusion that maybe what I need to fix is the visibility of the select state.

Testing my mockups

I tested three individuals with my new prototypes, one remotely and two in-person. I asked them to complete several tasks:

User testing script

  • Create an account and go through the onboarding tour.

  • Upload files to your Snapsack.

  • Add a new label to Pasta Place Logo.

  • Share your Japan 2017 photo album with a friend.

  • Edit Portfolio ideas and tag someone in a comment.

  • Star Portfolio ideas.

  • Move stray photo of uni sushi to your Camera folder.

  • Remove eyvind earle (copy) from your Snapsack.

  • Filter your view to show me only items labeled Branding and Complete.

Law of proximity (pt. 2)

One tester was felt “confused” about actions appearing on the other side when a file is selected. This was consistent with earlier wireframe problems regarding the proximity of Edit labels with the files.

Multiple label filtering

Another tester pointed out that filtering by multiple labels might be a “rare” use case, especially since I included the option to show items with all selected labels, which he considered “advanced.”

In spite of this feedback, users seemed, overall, open to using the organizational features of Snapsack.

This looks like it has a lot of color, colored labels, categories, which help me to organize better.

- Tester B on the labeling system

I like this idea of having to select a folder so I know where I’m uploading the file to.

- Tester C on folder selection upon file upload

Final

In previous iterations, I failed to use proximity to guide users in making an association between the target item and its actions. This was a recurring issue, whether it was about where Filter by label should go or where Edit labels should be placed.

Main changes

  • Proximity - Moved the file menu in a popup closer to the selected file.

  • Select state - Changed the thumbnail background to the palette’s primary yellow color and the checkboxes to the dark neutral color to make the selected file more visible

  • Filename - Decreased text size and added more space so that the text could wrap into two lines if needed.

  • Overflow menu icon - Added as a companion to the popup menu shown above. The label and star icons were moved to the top as a result of this change.

Conclusion

Organization in cloud storage apps can be improved. There is potential for labels to become a useful tool in terms of how people sort their files and folders.

Looking back

I considered what I could’ve done differently during the process.

What I would do differently

  • Focus on the organization aspect of the app. I got too caught up in other features like notes and previewing files that weren’t really fleshed out in the end.

  • Conduct more follow-up research about cloud storage app users and their organization habits. I feel that my survey data wasn’t sufficient due to low participant numbers and lack of foresight regarding the direction the product would take.

  • Consider options on making labels accessible. At the moment, labels are entirely reliant on the user’s ability to perceive colors accurately.

Building Snapsack was a learning lesson for me in many ways as well, whether about the design process or about the tools I used.

What I learned

  • Establishing goals for survey writing is important. My survey reflected that I was unsure about the direction I would go with my project. As a result, the results I got revealed too many gaps of information that I had to “fill in” myself by making assumptions about my audience.

  • Proximity is key. People are more likely to associate two separate items if they are in close proximity with one another. I was too caught up with other details that it slipped my mind to use such a basic principle of design.

  • Don’t use too many components in Figma. When designing my high-fidelity mockups, I nested components within components so that my working file would be as modular as possible (think classes in object-oriented programming). Copying one screen took the app upwards of 2 minutes to complete, and loading each screen of my prototype was awfully slow as well.

In spite of the setbacks, the positive comments I received post-testing reaffirmed that I was doing something right. A labeling system is a relatively simple feature that cloud storage apps could use to encourage their users to keep their digital spaces neat. Folders are “enough” but don’t provide much in the way of visual cues or non-linear access to files. Filtering by labels could be the answer to finding files easily.
