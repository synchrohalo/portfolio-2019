Social anxiety app

Inspired by the idea of using technology to connect lonely people, I set out to design an app that specializes in bringing together people with social anxiety. The result is a social platform akin to Meetup with an emphasis on positivity.

Summary

Team

Solo project

Roles

UX Designer, UI Designer, Illustrator

Tools

Paper & pen, Google forms, Whimsical, Adobe XD

Deliverables

User surveys, Competitive analysis, User personas, User stories, User flows, Sitemap, Low-fidelity wireframes, High-fidelity mockups, Graphic illustration

The problem

People with social anxiety tend to have trouble making friends because of the discomfort they feel in social situations, for a variety of reasons and insecurities. Issues related to this condition include a lack of trust between them and strangers, or simply not knowing what to say in a conversation with someone new.

The solution

Buddie is a place where locals with social anxiety can connect based on interests, bond over anxiety triumphs, and even meet in person if it gets that far. The app focuses on validation, mindfulness, and providing a sense of safety to give users an environment in which they can grow and overcome the hurdles that social anxiety creates.

Process

01 Research

Doing the homework

Research was the first item on my to-do list. I needed to get to know the demographic, learn more about similar apps in the market, and organize my findings in a meaningful way.

User survey

Initially, I faced two obstacles when seeking participants specific to my demographic: 1) it is a sensitive topic and 2) there is no way to confirm they actually have social anxiety. I turned to r/SampleSize on Reddit to get my survey out to a larger audience and accepted responses on a trust basis.

Key insights

  • Shared interests is considered the most important quality when forming a bond with a new friend.

  • Not knowing what to say is the number one fear participants have when communicating with new people.

  • Finally, participants are fueled by the power of words, whether through validation or encouragement to follow through with a meetup.

Another big problem that came to my attention was figuring out how to make the app a safe, comfortable space for users, especially for women and genderqueer people who are subject to sexual harassment. Forgetting to ask about gender and gender-related dynamics in a social app was a huge oversight on my part; I was grateful for the multiple instances of write-in answers pertaining to harassment and “creeps”.

I used my surveys to construct two distinct personas, one who prefers to meet people online and one who prefers to meet people offline.

Competitive analysis

As far as I could tell, there were no direct competitors to the app I had in mind, and so I looked to apps that were tangentially-related, namely ones focused on forming friendships or ones involving mental health and meditation.

In the end, I felt that Meetup.com best captured what I had in mind. Based on the research, people are more likely to make friends based on mutual interests, and so I wanted to create a similar platform using interest groups.

Though I didn’t conduct a formal analysis of it, I also looked to Reddit as inspiration for discussion boards, a feature that would target the persona type that doesn’t mind keeping relationships online.

Synthesizing information

I did a How Might We exercise to brainstorm solutions to the pain points I found from analyzing my survey results. From here, I created a list of user stories that I organized into categories based on similarity. These stories would form my MVP.

I simplified the tasks to create flows for each user story and turned the user stories into features to form a mock information architecture for my app.

Minimum viable product

  • Interest groups in your local area

  • Discussion boards within local interest groups

  • Events within local interest groups

  • Messaging through mutual approval

  • Self-reflection on an event a user has attended

  • Positive feedback for other users at an event a user has attended

02 Low-fidelity

Building a prototype

Wireframes

I tried a new approach here — right after making rough sketches, I made quick digital wireframes using Whimsical. With Whimsical, I could easily drag and drop ready-made components onto a mobile phone template. At the speed of sketching, I could build screens of a fidelity that requires less explanation than that of unrefined sketches.

Testing

I created a script that would walk testers through setup and other user stories I came up with in earlier phases.

Takeaways

  • Validation is key in helping people feel accepted.

  • Mindfulness should be exercised as soon as possible.

  • Use a positive tone for a warm experience.

Main changes

Remind user about Reflection sooner

One tester mentioned that mindfulness should be practiced as soon as possible so that the events are still fresh in the user's mind. I updated the notification to show that a reminder appears on the same day as when a user attends an event.

Full-fledged Reflection form

I created a more detailed version of the reflection form, including open-ended questions about how the user feels before, during, and after going to a social event.

Separate Affirmation feature

Based on the feedback, I added Affirmations to the main menu, where users can view received affirmations. I also made it a separate entity from the Reflection feature, so that users would be able to work on one thing at a time (versus having to do a reflection then write affirmations in the same flow).

When asked to block a user, one participant mentioned that the word “block” might come off as unfriendly, especially for an app targeting people with social anxiety. They went on to say that they’d wished there were more “positive” options listed alongside blocking so as to give the app a more welcoming feel. I kept this idea of positivity in mind as I improved upon my app.

03 High-fidelity

A prototype with polish

The first step in upgrading the visual design of the wireframes was to establish a simple style guide and get a handle on the branding.

High-fidelity screens

I applied my newly-established branding to create high-fidelity screens. Normally I create my prototypes screen by screen. This time, I started with making small components first, in an attempt to mimic the process behind atomic design (with the visual elements, at least).

As I started to put together each screen, I found that my components were either insufficient or too different from other iOS apps with similar patterns. Mobbin.design was a great reference for seeing how other apps tackle common patterns/features and helped me to figure out how to cement my “building blocks” together.

Testing

I asked users to complete the same tasks from the previous round of testing with the changes reflected in the newly-updated mockups.

Takeaways

  • Limit options as people with anxiety may have a harder time making a decision.

  • Focus on creating a positive tone and ways to make sure that the community is warm and inviting.

In more than one instance, testers indicated that the text fields used in the Reflection and Affirmation features can be overwhelming, and users may feel at a loss for what to type, even when given a prompt. This was possibly related to the insight from my original survey that not knowing what to say was one of the most common issues participants faced.

One tester cited an app called Moodnotes as a good example of mood journaling. One feature caught my eye, where users are prompted to answer how they are feeling by swiping the mouth’s curvature up or down.

This got me thinking about how I could more effectively get people to think about how they feel. I constructed a fixed list of the six basic emotions, each of which expands into a selection of more descriptive options (based on this emotion wheel).

Similarly, I modified Affirmations to include a selection of badges to present to event attendees of a user’s choice. Each badge is associated with a list of premade messages, akin to a greeting card, as suggested by a user in the previous round of testing.

Positivity

I hoped that the new Affirmations feature would address the request for an overwall positive tone. One tester felt that there should be some way to review received affirmations, and so I came up with a list of preset compliments to prevent the need for feedback.

Main changes

Reflection moods

With the improved Reflection feature, users can now select a feeling for each part of their event-going journey and track their moods throughout. Freewriting is still an option, but those prompts (and any step, for that matter) can simply be skipped.

Affirmation badges

Users can select a badge for event attendees they want to “affirm”. Along with preset words of encouragement, users may feel less pressure with these options at their disposal.

Testers post-update reacted positively to the changes. One mentioned that having options for both features “makes it easier” and likened it to LinkedIn’s feature of suggesting responses to messages. Another felt that the new affirmation badges “cut away from the pressure” of having to come up with something to write.

I feel a lot less pressure…no need to define how I’m feeling.

- Tester on the new Reflection moods

Conclusion

Outcome

At first I was afraid that my all of my ideas would culminate into an app so generalized, it wouldn’t remotely address any struggles associated with social anxiety.

In spite of the difficulties I faced mid-creation, however, I was happy to find that testers who did have social anxiety were receptive to the idea of Buddie and praised the inclusion of Reflection and Affirmation features, the two features which could set the app apart from other social platforms.

On the technical side, I was able to try out new workflows, such as using digital tools for ideating at the lo-fi level and trying my hand at creating components first while working on my hi-fi prototypes.

What I learned

  • Ask about gender (if applicable). With a social app that involves meeting strangers, sexual harassment is something that’s bound to be an issue. I wish I’d learned more about how my female and genderqueer demographic interacts online before proceeding with the app creation.

  • Apps aren’t always the answer. One tester recounted an experience during a social dancing class in which dancers use colored wristbands to indicate whether or not they are interested in dancing with a partner. This reminded me that you can’t solve everything with an app, and that you have to think outside of the box to find real-world solutions.

  • Think, but don’t think too hard. Sometimes thinking is the antithesis of getting things done. I was too afraid to make decisions that I wasted time thinking about what to do instead of taking action.

Next steps

If I were to continue with the project, I would:

  • Interview more women and genderqueer populations to learn more about how to create a safe space and better prevent harassment.

  • Create a more expansive Friend feature in which users can see which friends are going to which events.

  • Work carefully on copy and establish a brand language that feels friendly and accommodating.

  • Use more icons and illustrations to make things easier to process for users.

  • Simplify options, such as cutting down on the number of emoji reacts, so that users with anxiety feel less pressure when making a decision.

  • Expand on the features specific to social anxiety so that the app is more specialized for its target audience.

  • Test, test, test.

The list goes on. But, for now, I’m elated to have created something that not only touches on my own personal issues but could very well help someone else.
